## Learning Goals

By the end of this module, students should be able to:

- distinguish descriptive, causal, and forecasting questions;
- explain why causal claims require assumptions beyond correlations;
- identify cross-sectional, time-series, and panel data structures;
- distinguish estimand, estimator, and estimate;
- compute and interpret common moments of a distribution;
- work with conditional distributions and Bayes' rule;
- explain why estimates vary across samples;
- connect the law of large numbers and central limit theorem to empirical work;
- read the basic matrix notation used for OLS.

## Structure

1. [Econometrics and Questions](01-econometrics-questions.md)  
   What econometrics is, what kinds of questions it answers, and how to translate an economic question into variables.

2. [Data, Models, and Causality](02-data-causality.md)  
   Data structures, simple models, experiments, observational data, and the logic of causal inference.

3. [Random Variables and Moments](03-random-variables.md)  
   Random variables, distributions, mean, median, variance, standardization, covariance, and correlation.

4. [Conditional Distributions](04-conditional-distributions.md)  
   Conditional probabilities, Bayes' rule, conditional means, and the idea behind comparing groups.

5. [Random Sampling and Convergence](05-sampling-convergence.md)  
   Random samples, sampling variation, law of large numbers, and central limit theorem.

6. [Matrix Notation and Estimation](06-estimation.md)  
   Vector and matrix notation, estimands, estimators, estimates, and the preview of OLS.

## How To Use This Module In Class

The interactive panels are designed for live use:

- start with intuition from the graph;
- ask students to predict what happens when a slider moves;
- show the corresponding R or Python code;
- connect the code output back to the econometric concept.

For review, students can read the pages sequentially and rerun the code locally.
